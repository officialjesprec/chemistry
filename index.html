⚡ Redox Reactions

Part 1 — The Oxygen Story (Old School)

🔥 Oxidation = Gain of Oxygen

When magnesium ribbon burns, it gains oxygen — it has been oxidised.

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

🏭 Reduction = Loss of Oxygen

In the blast furnace, iron oxide loses oxygen to become iron metal — it has been reduced.

Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂

Part 2 — The Electron Story (Modern Definition)

OIL
Oxidation Is Loss
of electrons → charge becomes more positive
RIG
Reduction Is Gain
of electrons → charge becomes more negative

Oxidation Example

Sodium loses an electron → its charge increases from 0 to +1 → OXIDISED

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻

Reduction Example

Chlorine gains an electron → its charge decreases from 0 to -1 → REDUCED

Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

Part 3 — Oxidation Numbers (JAMB Favourite!)

🏅 The Golden Rules — Memorise These!

  • 1
    A free element (e.g. O₂, Na, Mg) has oxidation number = 0.
  • 2
    A simple ion — the oxidation number equals the charge. Na⁺ = +1, Cl⁻ = −1, Mg²⁺ = +2.
  • 3
    Oxygen in compounds = −2. Exception: In peroxides like H₂O₂ → −1
  • 4
    Hydrogen in compounds = +1. Exception: In metal hydrides like NaH → −1
  • 5
    For a neutral compound, the sum of all oxidation numbers = 0. For a polyatomic ion, the sum = charge of the ion.

Oxidation = INCREASE in oxidation number  |  Reduction = DECREASE in oxidation number

Part 4 — Oxidising & Reducing Agents

ProcessElectronsOxidation No.Agent InvolvedWhat happens to Agent
OXIDATIONLOSS of e⁻INCREASESReducing AgentIs OXIDISED
REDUCTIONGAIN of e⁻DECREASESOxidising AgentIs REDUCED

💡 Trick: The oxidising agent is the bully — it steals electrons from others (oxidising them) and gets REDUCED itself. The reducing agent is the giver — it donates electrons and gets OXIDISED.

Part 5 — JAMB Practice Questions

JAMB Type 1 — Definition
The process involving the loss of electrons is referred to as?
  • A. Reduction
  • B. Oxidation
  • C. Decomposition
  • D. Neutralization
Method: Use OIL RIG → Oxidation Is Loss of electrons. Answer = B.
JAMB Type 2 — Identify Oxidising Agent
In the reaction: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu, the oxidizing agent is?
  • A. Zn
  • B. CuSO₄
  • C. ZnSO₄
  • D. Cu
Step 1: Zn: 0 → +2 (INCREASE) = Oxidised.   Cu: +2 → 0 (DECREASE) = Reduced.
Step 2: The oxidising agent is the one that gets reduced. Cu²⁺ got reduced. The substance containing Cu²⁺ is CuSO₄. Answer = B.
JAMB Type 3 — Oxidation Numbers (Past Question)
What is the oxidation number of Chromium in K₂Cr₂O₇?
  • A. +2
  • B. +4
  • C. +6
  • D. +7
Setup: K₂Cr₂O₇ is neutral → sum = 0.
K = +1, so 2K = +2. O = −2, so 7O = −14. Let Cr = x, so 2Cr = 2x.
Equation: (+2) + (2x) + (−14) = 0 → 2x = 12 → x = +6. Answer = C.
JAMB Type 4 — Identify Redox Reaction
Which of the following reactions is a redox reaction?
  • A. NaCl + AgNO₃ → AgCl + NaNO₃
  • B. HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
  • C. 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
  • D. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
A & B & D: No change in oxidation states → NOT redox.
C: Na: 0 → +1 (INCREASE = Oxidised). Cl: 0 → −1 (DECREASE = Reduced). Changes found! Answer = C.

Air as a Mixture — Evidence

  • 1
    Variable Composition: The amount of water vapour and CO₂ changes from place to place — a compound has a fixed formula.
  • 2
    Retention of Properties: Oxygen still supports combustion, nitrogen remains unreactive.
  • 3
    No Heat Change: No significant energy is released when air components are mixed.
  • 4
    Easy Separation: Components can be separated by fractional distillation (a physical method).

Separation of Air — Fractional Distillation of Liquid Air

  • 1
    Purification: Filter dust; scrub out CO₂.
  • 2
    Drying: Remove water vapour.
  • 3
    Liquefaction: Compress and cool air until liquid.
  • 4
    Fractional Distillation: Nitrogen (b.p. −196°C) boils off first → Argon (b.p. −186°C) → Oxygen (b.p. −183°C) collected last.

B. Water

Formation & Composition

Water is formed when hydrogen burns in oxygen. Electrolysis proves H:O ratio = 2:1 by volume.

2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

At cathode: H₂ gas (double volume). At anode: O₂ gas.

Dissolved Atmospheric Gases

Dissolved O₂: Essential for aquatic organisms (fish respire through gills).

Dissolved CO₂: Required for photosynthesis by aquatic plants. Reacts with water → carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), dissolving minerals.

Hard and Soft Water

FeatureTemporary HardnessPermanent Hardness
CauseDissolved Ca(HCO₃)₂ and Mg(HCO₃)₂Dissolved CaSO₄, CaCl₂, MgSO₄
Remove by Boiling?YES — decomposes to insoluble CaCO₃NO — boiling has no effect
EquationCa(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂↑No reaction on boiling

Methods of Softening Hard Water

  • 1
    Boiling: Removes temporary hardness only.
  • 2
    Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃): Removes BOTH types. Ca²⁺ & Mg²⁺ precipitate as insoluble carbonates.
    CaSO₄ + Na₂CO₃ → CaCO₃↓ + Na₂SO₄
  • 3
    Ion-Exchange: Passes water through resin; swaps Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ for Na⁺. Removes both types.
  • 4
    Distillation: Produces pure water; removes all impurities but expensive for large scale.

Water Treatment for Town Supply

  • 1
    Screening & Storage: Screens remove large objects; solids settle in reservoir.
  • 2
    Sedimentation: Alum (Al₂(SO₄)₃) added as coagulant → fine particles clump into flocs and sink.
  • 3
    Filtration: Sand and gravel filters remove remaining fine particles.
  • 4
    Chlorination: Cl₂ or NaOCl kills bacteria and pathogens.
  • 5
    Fluoridation: Fluoride added in some areas to prevent tooth decay.

🧪 Non-metals & Their Compounds

A. Hydrogen (H₂)

Lab Preparation

React zinc granules with dilute HCl. Collect by downward displacement of air. Test: squeaky pop = pure H₂.

Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Commercial Production

From water gas: Steam over red-hot coke → CO + H₂. Then water-gas shift: CO + H₂O → CO₂ + H₂.

From cracking petroleum: Large hydrocarbons cracked into smaller molecules + H₂.

Uses of Hydrogen

Hydrogenation of oils (making margarine) · Haber process (ammonia) · Rocket fuel · Oxy-hydrogen welding torches

B. Chlorine (Cl₂) — Group 17 Halogen

Lab Preparation

Heat MnO₂ with concentrated HCl. Collect by upward displacement of air (denser than air). Dry with conc. H₂SO₄.

MnO₂(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl₂ + Cl₂(g) + 2H₂O

Industrial Preparation (Electrolysis of Brine)

2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)

Anode (+): 2Cl⁻ − 2e⁻ → Cl₂(g)

Cathode (−): 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂(g) + 2OH⁻

By-products: NaOH solution and H₂ gas.

Properties of Chlorine

  • P
    Greenish-yellow gas, ~2.5× denser than air, pungent/suffocating, poisonous.
  • C
    Reaction with water: Cl₂ + H₂O ⇌ HCl + HOCl (chlorine water). HOCl → HCl + [O] — nascent oxygen bleaches permanently by oxidation.
  • C
    With cold dilute NaOH: Forms bleach (sodium chlorate(I)).

Uses: Water sterilisation · Bleaching paper & textiles · Making HCl, PVC, insecticides.

C. Hydrogen Chloride & Hydrochloric Acid

Lab Preparation of HCl Gas

NaCl(s) + H₂SO₄(l) → NaHSO₄(s) + HCl(g) [low temp]

Collect by upward delivery (denser than air). Dry through conc. H₂SO₄.

Key fact: Dry HCl does NOT affect litmus. Only in moisture does it form an acid and turn blue litmus red.

HCl + NH₃ → NH₄Cl (dense white fumes — test for either gas)

Ozone (O₃) — Allotrope of Oxygen

Allotropy = existence of an element in two or more physical forms in the same physical state.

Ozone forms from O₂ under UV light or electrical discharge: 3O₂ → 2O₃

Importance: The ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs harmful UV radiation, protecting life from skin cancer and crop damage.

E. Sulphur

Allotropes of Sulphur

Rhombic sulphur: Octahedral crystals; stable below 96°C. Monoclinic sulphur: Needle-shaped; stable above 96°C. Both are S₈ ring molecules.

Uses: Vulcanisation of rubber · H₂SO₄ manufacture · Gunpowder & matches · Skin ointments (pharmaceutical).

Sulphur(IV) Oxide — SO₂

Lab: Na₂SO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + SO₂↑

Acidic oxide: SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (sulphurous acid)

Bleaching agent (by reduction — temporary, colour returns in air).

Reducing agent: decolourises acidified KMnO₄.

Sulphuric Acid H₂SO₄ — Contact Process

  • 1
    S + O₂ → SO₂ (burning sulphur)
  • 2
    2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (V₂O₅ catalyst, 450°C)
  • 3
    SO₃ absorbed in conc. H₂SO₄ → Oleum → diluted to get H₂SO₄

Also acts as: Oxidising agent (hot conc.) and Dehydrating agent (chars sugar).

💨 Nitrogen & Compounds

A. Nitrogen — Lab Preparation

Heat ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl) with sodium nitrite (NaNO₂) solution.

NH₄Cl(aq) + NaNO₂(aq) → NaCl(aq) + N₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

N₂ is also obtained industrially by fractional distillation of liquid air (boils off first at −196°C).

B. Ammonia (NH₃)

Lab Preparation

Heat ammonium salt with a strong base (slaked lime).

2NH₄Cl(s) + Ca(OH)₂(s) → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O + 2NH₃(g)

Collect by downward displacement of air (less dense). Cannot collect over water (too soluble).

Haber Process (Industrial)

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92.4 kJ/mol
  • P
    Pressure: 200 atm (high pressure favours fewer gas molecules)
  • T
    Temperature: 450°C (compromise — yield vs. rate)
  • C
    Catalyst: Finely divided iron (+ promoters Al₂O₃ and K₂O)

Properties & Uses of Ammonia

Colourless gas with sharp pungent odour. Extremely soluble → forms alkaline solution: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

Uses: Nitrogenous fertilizers (urea, ammonium sulphate) · Nitric acid (Ostwald process) · Refrigerant · Cleaning agent.

F. The Nitrogen Cycle

The nitrogen cycle converts atmospheric N₂ into usable forms and back. Nitrogen is essential for amino acids, proteins, and DNA.

  • 1
    Nitrogen Fixation: N₂ → NH₃ by Rhizobium bacteria (legume root nodules), lightning, or Haber process.
  • 2
    Nitrification: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ by nitrifying bacteria. Plants absorb nitrates.
  • 3
    Assimilation: Plants use NO₃⁻ to build proteins. Animals eat plants to get nitrogen.
  • 4
    Ammonification: Decomposers break down dead matter → NH₃ released back into soil.
  • 5
    Denitrification: Denitrifying bacteria convert NO₃⁻ → N₂ gas, completing the cycle.

Environmental note: Overuse of fertilizers → excess nitrates → eutrophication in water bodies.

🌫️ Environmental Pollution

Pollution is the introduction of contaminants into the environment that cause adverse change to ecosystems and human health.

Water Pollution

Sewage Pollution

Domestic waste discharged into water contains pathogens → waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid, dysentery).

Decomposition depletes dissolved O₂ → fish and aquatic life die (eutrophication).

Oil Pollution

Tanker spills, offshore drilling leaks (e.g. Niger Delta), pipeline bursts.

Oil slick blocks O₂ from dissolving → suffocates aquatic life. Coats feathers of birds, fur of marine mammals → death. Destroys mangroves and beaches.

Soil Pollution — Biodegradable vs. Non-Biodegradable

FeatureBiodegradableNon-Biodegradable
DefinitionBroken down by microorganisms into harmless substancesCannot be broken down by natural processes (persists hundreds of years)
ExamplesSewage, animal waste, vegetable matter, paper, woodPlastics, nylon, polythene bags, heavy metals (Pb, Hg), DDT, glass

Control of Environmental Pollution

  • 1
    Regulatory Measures: Laws banning CFCs, setting vehicle and industrial emission standards.
  • 2
    Technological Solutions: Catalytic converters in cars; scrubbers and electrostatic precipitators on chimneys; cleaner fuels.
  • 3
    Waste Management: The 3 R's — Reduce, Reuse, Recycle. Treat sewage and industrial waste before discharge.
  • 4
    Renewable Energy: Solar, wind, hydroelectric power → reduce fossil fuel combustion.
  • 5
    Public Awareness: Educate on proper waste disposal, reducing plastic use, reporting oil spills.
  • 6
    Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA): Mandatory for major projects (oil drilling, dam construction) before commencement.

C. Sodium & Its Compounds

NaOH — Caustic Soda (Electrolysis of Brine)

2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂   (electrolysis)

Anode (+): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻  |  Cathode (−): 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻

Properties: White deliquescent solid · Strong alkali · Soapy to touch · Reacts with acids (neutralisation).

Uses: Soap manufacture · Paper processing · Petroleum refining · Drain cleaners · Bayer process (alumina).

Key Reactions of NaOH

  • Al
    2Al + 2NaOH + 6H₂O → 2NaAl(OH)₄ + 3H₂  Sodium tetrahydroxoaluminate
  • Zn
    Zn + 2NaOH + 2H₂O → Na₂Zn(OH)₄ + H₂  Sodium tetrahydroxozincate
  • Pb²⁺

  • NH₄⁺
    NH₄Cl + NaOH → NaCl + NH₃↑ + H₂O  Test for ammonium ion

Na₂CO₃ — Washing Soda (Solvay Process)

Raw materials: NaCl (brine) · NH₃ (ammonia) · CaCO₃ (limestone) · CO₂

  • 1
    NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NH₄HCO₃
  • 2
    NH₄HCO₃ + NaCl → NaHCO₃↓ + NH₄Cl
  • 3
    2NaHCO₃ →(heat) Na₂CO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O
  • 4
    2NH₄Cl + Ca(OH)₂ → 2NH₃ + CaCl₂ + 2H₂O  NH₃ recycled

Uses: Glass manufacture · Water softening (precipitates Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺) · Soap/detergent making · Paper and textile industries.

NaHCO₃ — Baking Soda

Intermediate product of Solvay process. Decomposes on heating:

2NaHCO₃ →(Δ) Na₂CO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O

Uses: Baking (CO₂ raises dough) · Fire extinguishers · Antacid (neutralises stomach acid) · Effervescent drinks.

NaCl — Common Salt

Occurrence: Sea water (~3%) · Rock salt deposits · Brine wells.

Uses: Human nutrition · Food preservation · Raw material for Cl₂ and NaOH · De-icing roads · Water treatment.

E. Aluminium

Most abundant metal in Earth's crust. Main ore: Bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O).

Bayer Process — Purifying Bauxite

  • 1
    Digestion: Crushed bauxite + hot NaOH → Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH + 3H₂O → 2NaAl(OH)₄
  • 2
    Filtration: Impurities (Fe₂O₃, SiO₂) removed as red mud.
  • 3
    Precipitation: Cool and seed → NaAl(OH)₄ → Al(OH)₃↓ + NaOH
  • 4
    Calcination: 2Al(OH)₃ →(Δ) Al₂O₃ + 3H₂O

Hall-Héroult Process — Extracting Aluminium

Electrolysis of Al₂O₃ dissolved in molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) at ~950°C.

Cryolite: Lowers melting point and increases conductivity.

Anodes: Carbon/graphite — consumed by O₂, must be replaced.

Cathode (−): Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
Anode (+): 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
Overall: 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂

Key Chemical Properties of Aluminium

  • Air
    Forms protective Al₂O₃ layer — prevents further reaction (passivation).
  • Acid
    2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂   (passivated by concentrated HNO₃)
  • Alkali
    2Al + 2NaOH + 6H₂O → 2NaAl(OH)₄ + 3H₂  Amphoteric!
  • Thermite
    2Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe + heat  Very exothermic — used in thermite welding

Uses: Aircraft/vehicle parts · Overhead power cables · Cooking foil · Window frames · Mirrors · Aluminium paint (reflects heat).

E. Reversible Reactions & Dynamic Equilibrium

Reversible Reactions

Reactions that can proceed in both forward and backward directions simultaneously, represented by ⇌.

They do not go to completion — they reach a state of balance where both reactants and products are present.

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)   (Haber)
N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)   (colourless ⇌ brown)
CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

Dynamic Equilibrium

A state where the rate of forward reaction = rate of backward reaction. Both reactions still occur — there is just no net change.

  • ✓
    Concentrations of all species remain constant
  • ✓
    Requires a closed system
  • ✓
    Same equilibrium reached from either direction
  • ✓
    Position changes with temperature

F. Le Chatelier's Principle

📜 The Principle

"If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, pressure, or temperature, the equilibrium shifts in a direction that tends to counteract the effect of that change."

Factor ChangedDirection of ShiftEffect on K
Increase reactant concentration→ Right (more products formed)No change
Decrease reactant concentration← Left (more reactants formed)No change
Increase pressure→ Side with FEWER gas moleculesNo change
Decrease pressure← Side with MORE gas moleculesNo change
Increase temperature→ Endothermic directionChanges (K increases for endo)
Decrease temperature→ Exothermic directionChanges (K decreases for endo)
Add catalystNo shift in positionNo change — only rate increases

Worked Example — N₂O₄ ⇌ 2NO₂ System

N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)   ΔH = +58 kJ mol⁻¹

N₂O₄: Colourless  |  NO₂: Brown — colour change makes this a perfect observable example!

ChangeObservationDirection of Shift
Increase pressureColour fades (less brown)← Left (towards 1 mole N₂O₄)
Decrease pressureColour deepens (more brown)→ Right (towards 2 moles NO₂)
Increase temperatureColour deepens (more brown)→ Right (endothermic direction)
Decrease temperatureColour fades (less brown)← Left (exothermic direction)
Add catalystEquilibrium reached faster, no colour changeNo shift

G. Equilibrium Constant (K)

For the general reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ   (concentrations at equilibrium in mol/dm³)
Value of KMeaning
K > 1Equilibrium favours products — reaction goes nearly to completion
K = 1Significant amounts of both reactants and products present
K < 1Equilibrium favours reactants — very little product formed

KEY RULE: Only temperature changes the value of K. Concentration, pressure, and catalyst do NOT change K.

H. Industrial Applications

Haber Process — Ammonia

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)   ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
  • P
    200 atm — high pressure favours forward reaction (4 moles → 2 moles)
  • T
    450°C — compromise: low temp favours yield but rate too slow
  • C
    Iron catalyst — speeds up attainment of equilibrium
  • ↑
    NH₃ continuously removed → shifts equilibrium right

Contact Process — H₂SO₄

2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g)   ΔH = −196 kJ/mol
  • P
    1–2 atm moderate pressure (3 moles → 2 moles)
  • T
    400–450°C — compromise between yield and rate
  • C
    V₂O₅ vanadium(V) oxide catalyst
  • ↑
    Excess O₂ used to shift equilibrium right

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

  • ❌
    "At equilibrium, reactions stop" — WRONG! Both reactions continue at equal rates.
  • ❌
    "Catalyst increases yield" — WRONG! Catalyst only speeds up; it does not change yield or K.
  • ❌
    "Pressure always affects equilibrium" — WRONG! Only if gas moles are unequal on both sides.
  • ❌
    "Adding more solid changes equilibrium" — WRONG! Solids have constant concentration; they do not shift equilibrium.
