Acids — 10 Examples

An acid is a substance that donates H⁺ ions (protons) in solution, has a pH < 7, turns blue litmus red, and reacts with metals to produce hydrogen gas.

⚡ Strong Acids

  • 1.
    Hydrochloric Acid — HCl
    Found in stomach; very corrosive
  • 2.
    Sulphuric Acid — H₂SO₄
    Used in car batteries; industrial chemical
  • 3.
    Nitric Acid — HNO₃
    Used in making fertilizers & explosives
  • 4.
    Hydrobromic Acid — HBr
    Strong acid, highly corrosive
  • 5.
    Perchloric Acid — HClO₄
    Strongest common acid

🌿 Weak / Organic Acids

  • 6.
    Ethanoic Acid — CH₃COOH
    Acetic acid; found in vinegar
  • 7.
    Citric Acid — C₆H₈O₇
    Found in citrus fruits like orange & lemon
  • 8.
    Carbonic Acid — H₂CO₃
    In fizzy drinks (CO₂ + H₂O)
  • 9.
    Methanoic Acid — HCOOH
    Formic acid; found in ant stings
  • 10.
    Lactic Acid — C₃H₆O₃
    Produced in muscles during exercise

Bases — 10 Examples

A base is a substance that accepts H⁺ ions or donates OH⁻ ions. Soluble bases are called alkalis. pH > 7, turns red litmus blue, feels soapy.

💧 Alkalis (Soluble Bases)

  • 1.
    Sodium Hydroxide — NaOH
    Caustic soda; used in soap making
  • 2.
    Potassium Hydroxide — KOH
    Caustic potash; used in batteries
  • 3.
    Calcium Hydroxide — Ca(OH)₂
    Slaked lime; used in whitewashing walls
  • 4.
    Ammonium Hydroxide — NH₄OH
    Ammonia solution; used in cleaning
  • 5.
    Barium Hydroxide — Ba(OH)₂
    Strong base used in titrations

🪨 Insoluble Bases

  • 6.
    Copper(II) Hydroxide — Cu(OH)₂
    Blue precipitate; insoluble base
  • 7.
    Iron(III) Hydroxide — Fe(OH)₃
    Reddish-brown precipitate
  • 8.
    Zinc Oxide — ZnO
    Amphoteric base; used in calamine lotion
  • 9.
    Magnesium Hydroxide — Mg(OH)₂
    Milk of magnesia; antacid medicine
  • 10.
    Aluminium Hydroxide — Al(OH)₃
    Amphoteric; used in antacids

Salts — 10 Examples

A salt is formed when the H⁺ of an acid is replaced (fully or partially) by a metal ion or ammonium ion. Produced in neutralisation reactions (Acid + Base → Salt + Water).

🧂 Normal Salts

  • 1.
    Sodium Chloride — NaCl
    Common table salt; from HCl + NaOH
  • 2.
    Calcium Carbonate — CaCO₃
    Limestone, chalk, marble
  • 3.
    Copper(II) Sulphate — CuSO₄
    Blue vitriol; used in electroplating
  • 4.
    Ammonium Nitrate — NH₄NO₃
    Fertilizer; from HNO₃ + NH₃
  • 5.
    Potassium Chloride — KCl
    Sylvite; fertilizer & food additive

✨ More Salts

  • 6.
    Sodium Carbonate — Na₂CO₃
    Washing soda; water softener
  • 7.
    Iron(II) Sulphate — FeSO₄
    Green vitriol; used in ink making
  • 8.
    Zinc Sulphate — ZnSO₄
    White vitriol; in fertilizers
  • 9.
    Silver Nitrate — AgNO₃
    Used in photography & medicine
  • 10.
    Sodium Bicarbonate — NaHCO₃
    Baking soda; acid salt from H₂CO₃

Soluble Substances — 10 Examples

A soluble substance dissolves completely in a solvent (usually water) to form a homogeneous solution. Solubility depends on temperature, pressure, and the nature of solute/solvent ("like dissolves like").

💧 Soluble in Water

  • 1.
    Sodium Chloride — NaCl
    Table salt; dissolves readily in water
  • 2.
    Sugar (Sucrose) — C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁
    Highly soluble in hot or cold water
  • 3.
    Copper(II) Sulphate — CuSO₄
    Forms deep blue solution
  • 4.
    Potassium Nitrate — KNO₃
    Saltpetre; very soluble, increases with temp
  • 5.
    Ethanol — C₂H₅OH
    Completely miscible with water

🌊 More Solubles

  • 6.
    Ammonium Chloride — NH₄Cl
    Sal ammoniac; dissolves endothermically
  • 7.
    Sodium Hydroxide — NaOH
    Very soluble; dissolution is exothermic
  • 8.
    Urea — CO(NH₂)₂
    Highly soluble; important in fertilizers
  • 9.
    Glucose — C₆H₁₂O₆
    Dissolves in water; transported in blood
  • 10.
    Potassium Permanganate — KMnO₄
    Dissolves to give purple solution

Solvents — 10 Examples

A solvent is the dissolving medium in a solution — the substance present in greater quantity. Water is the "universal solvent". Polar solvents dissolve ionic/polar solutes; non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar solutes.

🌍 Polar Solvents

  • 1.
    Water — H₂O
    Universal solvent; dissolves ionic compounds
  • 2.
    Ethanol — C₂H₅OH
    Dissolves both polar & some non-polar substances
  • 3.
    Methanol — CH₃OH
    Wood alcohol; polar, miscible with water
  • 4.
    Acetone — (CH₃)₂CO
    Nail polish remover; dissolves organic compounds
  • 5.
    Acetic Acid — CH₃COOH
    Glacial acetic acid used as polar solvent

🛢️ Non-Polar Solvents

  • 6.
    Benzene — C₆H₆
    Dissolves fats, oils, rubber; carcinogenic
  • 7.
    Tetrachloromethane — CCl₄
    Carbon tetrachloride; non-polar solvent
  • 8.
    Hexane — C₆H₁₄
    Used to extract oils from seeds
  • 9.
    Diethyl Ether — C₄H₁₀O
    Used in labs; historically as anaesthetic
  • 10.
    Kerosene — (Hydrocarbon mixture)
    Dissolves grease, wax, and non-polar substances

Organic Chemistry — Homologous Series

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds containing only Carbon and Hydrogen. The three main series below differ in their bond types and general formulas.

🟢 Alkanes

General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Single C–C bonds only (saturated). Suffix: -ane. Used as fuels. Undergo substitution reactions.

1MethaneCH₄
2EthaneC₂H₆
3PropaneC₃H₈
4ButaneC₄H₁₀
5PentaneC₅H₁₂

🟠 Alkenes

General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ
One C=C double bond (unsaturated). Suffix: -ene. Undergo addition reactions. Decolourise bromine water.

1EtheneC₂H₄
2PropeneC₃H₆
3ButeneC₄H₈
4PenteneC₅H₁₀
5HexeneC₆H₁₂

🔴 Alkynes

General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
One C≡C triple bond (highly unsaturated). Suffix: -yne. Very reactive; also undergo addition reactions.

1EthyneC₂H₂
2PropyneC₃H₄
3ButyneC₄H₆
4PentyneC₅H₈
5HexyneC₆H₁₀
